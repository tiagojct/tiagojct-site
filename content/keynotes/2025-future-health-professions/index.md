*Universidade do Porto Júnior* brings secondary-school students to the university for hands-on sessions across faculties. This annual lecture at the Faculty of Medicine introduces the full scope of health professions — not medicine alone, but the allied and informatics-facing roles that sustain and increasingly define how clinical care works.

The lecture begins with the breadth of the health workforce: who does what, why the specialisation exists, and what the professional landscape looks like in practice. It then turns to the part that changes most visibly over a career: how digital tools, data systems, and AI applications are entering clinical environments, what they actually do well (pattern recognition in high-volume structured data), and what they do not do (explain themselves, account for the patient in the room, take responsibility for an error).

The thread running through the session is a question students rarely hear put directly: if you choose health as a career, you will spend your professional life working alongside systems that were not designed for your context and tools that will change faster than any curriculum. The question is not whether AI will affect your field. The question is whether you will understand it well enough to push back when you need to, and advocate for patients when the system defaults to the wrong answer.
