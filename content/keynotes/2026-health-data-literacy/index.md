The promise of digital health data is that it makes information visible, shareable, and actionable across a care team. The reality is that a team of clinicians — nurses, physicians, physiotherapists, pharmacists — generates and consumes data from radically different sources, in radically different formats, using radically different professional languages. Clinical notes in free text. Laboratory results in structured values. Imaging reports. Physiological scores. Patient-reported outcomes. Medication adherence logs.

These professionals rarely share a common vocabulary for the data they produce. When a nurse documents "the patient cannot keep up with people of the same age" and a physician writes "mMRC grade 2 dyspnoea," they are describing the same clinical reality in different languages. This is not a technical problem. It is a language problem — and it is the reason that interprofessional communication about data remains so difficult.

The AICA framework — Access, Interpret, Communicate, Act — proposes health data literacy as a collective competency for interprofessional teams rather than a set of individual technical skills. The framework distinguishes between shared competencies (those every team member needs regardless of role) and differentiated competencies (those specific to a profession's data-generating and data-consuming practice). Only the collective working of all four stages — accessing data, interpreting it in clinical context, communicating it across professions and to patients, and acting on it — produces data-informed care.

The regulatory context makes this more than a pedagogical concern. The EU AI Act, in force from February 2025, requires AI literacy for all operators of high-risk AI systems in health. The European Health Data Space, in force from August 2026, mandates secondary use of health data under conditions that require professionals to understand what they are working with. Together these regulations transform health data literacy from an optional skill into a legal obligation — and interprofessional teams are the unit in which it must be practiced.
