The HEADS (Health Education and Digital Solutions) seminar series brought together researchers working at the intersection of clinical practice and informatics. This session uses the arc of a research career as a case study in how medical informatics problems get identified, framed, and eventually built into usable systems.

The talk begins with the clinical problem: exhaled nitric oxide (FeNO) as a biomarker for airway inflammation in asthma. FeNO values are continuous, influenced by multiple confounders, and interpreted against population-derived reference values that were poorly standardised for much of the time they were in clinical use. The decision to build a decision support tool was not driven by an interest in technology — it was driven by the observation that clinicians were applying the same fixed thresholds to patients who differed substantially in age, sex, height, and ethnicity. That mismatch had a clinical cost.

The second thread follows the move to mobile health. AIRDOC began as an attempt to extend the same logic — evidence-based, individualised, structured — from the clinical encounter into the patient's daily life. Building a mobile ecosystem that captures lung function data, delivers coaching content, and surfaces clinically relevant signals required grappling with a different set of constraints: hardware variability, connectivity gaps, adherence patterns, and the difference between a system that works in a trial and one that holds up in routine care.

The seminar closes with the methodological question that connects both projects: how do you evaluate a clinical AI tool? Not whether its algorithm is accurate on a test set, but whether it changes decisions, and whether those changed decisions benefit patients. The answer requires external validation, real-world evidence, human factors work, and a tolerance for slow, unglamorous results. That is still the centre of the work.
