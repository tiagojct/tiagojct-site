Technology in medicine is never a simple addition. Every tool that enters clinical practice changes what doctors and nurses can do — and changes what they must do differently. The stethoscope gave clinicians the ability to hear what was previously inaudible, but it also introduced the first instrument to come between a physician and a patient. The X-ray made the body transparent without surgery, but was adopted before the harms of radiation were understood. The electronic health record made information accessible and shareable, but introduced bureaucratic burden, documentation fatigue, and interoperability problems that persist to this day.

This talk uses Neil Postman's framework — borrowed from his critique of technological optimism in *Technopoly* — to argue that the question worth asking of any new technology in health is not "what does this do?" but "what does this undo?" The history of medicine shows that every technology arrives carrying both a blessing and a burden, and that the burden only becomes visible long after the blessing has been celebrated.

Drawing on these historical precedents, the talk examines where AI in health stands today. Diagnostic support, administrative automation, and public health surveillance represent genuine capabilities. But alongside them run three familiar risks: bias introduced by training data that does not represent the population served; opacity in systems whose decision logic cannot be inspected; and the seduction of convenience, which makes adoption feel like progress even when it dissolves professional judgment.

The talk closes with a challenge: that the most important question to ask of any AI system presented for clinical use is not "does this work?" but "what does this take from us?" The technologies that survive in medicine will be those that clinicians and patients decide are worth the cost — which requires asking the question before the technology has already answered it.
