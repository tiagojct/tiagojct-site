The respiratory function laboratory is not disappearing. It is, however, moving — partially and unevenly — into the home, the primary care clinic, and the patient's pocket. Telemedicine platforms, home spirometry devices, oximetry wearables, and remote monitoring services are already clinically deployed in some contexts. What is changing for the professional sitting in the middle of these systems?

The talk takes the respiratory physiologist as a case study in a wider transformation of allied health professions. Clinical roles built around performing tests in controlled environments are being asked to extend across uncontrolled ones. That extension is technical — new devices, new connectivity, new data formats — but the core challenge is interpretive. A telemedicine result is not the same as a lab result, and the training required to say which one you are holding is not the same as the training required to perform the test.

Three threads run through the talk: how quality assurance principles extend (or fail to extend) to remote settings; what data literacy looks like in a department where some results arrive as structured exports and others arrive as image files or free text; and what professional advocacy looks like when clinical informatics decisions are made at institutional level and the physiologist is an end-user rather than a designer.
